# Setting Up Your Station

**Activates when you're choosing tools or organizing the space you cook in** —
not mid-cook, but when deciding what lives on your counter and in your drawers.
Your station is infrastructure. Every cook runs on it, so its quality compounds:
a good setup makes every future meal faster, and a cluttered one taxes every
meal forever.

## The Iron Law

**Few good tools, kept sharp and in reach, beat many cheap ones.** A drawer you
have to dig through is slower than a hook with the three things you actually use.
Capability per tool, not tool count, is the metric.

## Buy fewer, better tools

A sharp chef's knife, a heavy cutting board, one good pan, a pot, a couple of
spoons, tongs — this handles the overwhelming majority of all cooking. Spend
your budget *deepening* that core, not widening it. A great knife outperforms a
block of mediocre ones, lasts decades, and you actually maintain it because it's
the one you reach for. Ten cheap knives is not ten capabilities; it's ten things
to store, none of which you trust.

The cheap-tool trap is false economy: each one underperforms, you don't bond
with any of them, and collectively they cost more in money and drawer space than
the few good tools would have. Quality concentrates capability where you can
actually use it.

## Resist the unitasker

Before acquiring any single-purpose gadget, ask: **does a skill or a tool I
already own do this?** The garlic press is replaced by the side of your knife.
The avocado slicer, the strawberry huller, the egg separator, the banana cutter
— each does one narrow job that a knife, a spoon, and a little technique already
cover. The gadget isn't free even if it's cheap: it costs drawer space, it costs
the decision of whether to use it, and it costs the chance to build the skill
that would've made it unnecessary.

A unitasker earns its place only when it does something genuinely hard to do
otherwise *and* you'll use it often — a good can opener, a fish spatula, a
scale. One-job tools you reach for weekly are infrastructure. One-job tools you
bought for a single recipe are clutter that becomes a
`supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry` line item.

## Skill is the tool that fits in no drawer

The deepest version of this: technique replaces equipment. Knife skills replace
half the gadget aisle. Knowing how to tell doneness by touch replaces a drawer
of single-use thermometers. Investing in skill is investing in a tool that's
always with you, never needs storage, and improves with use. Reach for the
technique before the purchase.

## Keep it sharp and in reach

A good tool neglected is a cheap tool. A dull "good knife" is more dangerous and
slower than a sharp modest one — maintenance is part of owning it, not optional.
And the best tool stored badly is a bad tool: if it's buried, you won't use it
under pressure. The things you use every cook live within one reach of where you
stand. This is the same discipline as the REFACTOR step of
`supper-powers:test-driven-dinner` — a station reset to its fastest state — but
applied to the durable setup rather than the per-course cleanup.

## Anti-Rationalization

- "But it's only a few dollars." → Price isn't the cost. Drawer space, decision
  overhead, and the un-built skill are the cost, and they're charged every time
  you open that drawer. Cheap clutter is still clutter.
- "It might be useful someday." → Someday-tools are speculative inventory. Buy
  for the cooking you actually do, not the cooking you imagine. The someday tool
  is a `paying-down-the-pantry` write-off bought in advance.
- "More options means more capability." → More options means more to search
  through and maintain. Capability comes from a few tools you've mastered, not
  many you half-know. A widened drawer is not a deepened skill.
- "I need the specialized one to do this right." → Usually you need the
  technique. Try the knife-and-skill path before the gadget; build the
  capability that fits in no drawer.

**About to buy a single-purpose gadget? Stop. Name the skill or existing tool
that already does the job.** If you can't, and you'll use it often, fine — buy
it on purpose. If you can, build the skill instead. Violating the letter of this
rule is violating the spirit of it.

## Boundary

This skill governs the durable setup — what tools you own and how the station is
organized. It does not run the per-meal prep (`supper-powers:using-mise-en-place`),
it does not do the per-course station reset (the REFACTOR step of
`supper-powers:test-driven-dinner`), and it does not decide build-vs-buy for
*ingredients* (`supper-powers:bake-or-bakery`, which is the same instinct
applied to food rather than tools). Stay in scope: few good tools, lean station,
skill over gadget.

## User instructions always win

If your human partner wants the gadget — they like it, it sparks joy, they just
want it — let them have it. The user is in control. Name the lean-station case
once ("the knife does this too, for what it's worth"), then drop it. Their
kitchen, their call.
