# Requesting Table Review

**Activates between courses, before a plate goes out.** The cook is the worst
judge of their own dish — palate fatigue is real, you stopped tasting the salt
an hour ago, and you are emotionally invested in the thing you just made. Get a
second mouth.

## The Iron Law

**A critical issue blocks service. Full stop.** Raw chicken does not go out
because the cook is tired and the guests are waiting. Severity gates the pass.

## Get a real reviewer

Hand a clean spoon to someone who did not cook this course. A second cook, a
hungry housemate, anyone with a working palate and permission to be honest.
The reviewer's job is not to be nice. It is to catch what you can no longer
taste. "It's fine" is not a review; push for a real read.

## Triage by severity

Every issue gets a severity. This is the whole point — not all problems are
equal, and treating a nit like a crisis (or a crisis like a nit) is the failure
mode.

- **CRITICAL — blocks service.** Unsafe or inedible: undercooked meat, an
  allergen that shouldn't be there, badly burnt, wildly oversalted to the point
  of harm. Nothing leaves the kitchen until this is resolved. Re-cook, remake,
  or pull the dish.
- **MAJOR — fix before serving.** Genuinely off but safe: under-seasoned to
  bland, wrong texture, a component cold that should be hot. Fixable at the
  pass. Fix it, then re-review.
- **MINOR — log it, serve anyway.** Could be better, isn't wrong: "a touch more
  acid would lift it," "I'd plate the herbs higher." Note it for next time
  (this is a candidate for `supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry`'s next-cook
  list) and send the plate.

## Review against the menu, not against perfection

The bar is the **agreed menu** from `supper-powers:brainstorming-supper`, not
some platonic ideal dish. If they asked for a quick weeknight bowl, "this isn't
restaurant-plated" is not a valid major — it met the spec. Review what was
promised, not what you wish you'd promised.

## Both sides of the pass

This skill has two roles. As the **cook requesting review**, you ask early and
often and you do not argue with critical findings — you fix them. As the
**reviewer**, you taste honestly, you assign severity instead of vibes, and you
separate "unsafe" from "I'd do it differently." Don't gold-plate someone else's
dish with majors that are really your personal minors.

## Anti-Rationalization

- "I tasted it myself, it's fine." → You have palate fatigue and a conflict of
  interest. Both disqualify you as the final judge. Get a second mouth.
- "The guests are waiting, no time to review." → A review is one bite and ten
  seconds. Re-cooking a course you sent out raw is twenty minutes and an
  apology. There is time.
- "It's basically done, the issue is small." → Then it's a MINOR and you log it
  and serve. If you're not sure it's small, it isn't — review it.
- "They'll be polite about it anyway." → Politeness at the table is not a pass.
  You wanted it good, not survived. Get the honest read before it's too late.

**About to serve a course no one else has checked? Stop. Hand over the spoon.**
A critical finding overrides the clock every time. Violating the letter of this
rule is violating the spirit of it.

## Boundary

This skill judges a finished-enough course against the menu. It does not set
the menu (`supper-powers:brainstorming-supper`), it does not do the seasoning
loop that gets a course to "done" (`supper-powers:test-driven-dinner`), and it
does not clean the station afterward (that's the REFACTOR step of
test-driven-dinner). Stay in scope: review, severity, gate the pass.

## User instructions always win

If your human partner says "skip the review, send it" — send it. The user is in
control. Name any critical concern once and plainly ("the thigh meat may still
be pink"), then defer. Their kitchen, their call.
