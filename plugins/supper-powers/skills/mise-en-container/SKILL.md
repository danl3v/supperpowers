# Mise en Container

**Activates when you have prep time you could invest, not just food you need to
store.** This is not a leftovers skill. Leftovers are what's left over;
containers are what you build *on purpose* so that next week's dinner is a
five-minute assembly job instead of a forty-minute cook from raw.

The insight: a container is not a place to put food. It is a reusable building
block you grab off the shelf. Stock the shelf with the right blocks and cooking
stops being manufacturing and becomes composition.

## The Iron Law

**Prep for composition, not just preservation.** The question is never only
"will this keep?" It's "what can I *build* from this in thirty seconds next
week?" A container earns its shelf space by being a grab-and-go primitive that
collapses a future meal, not by merely not spoiling.

## The abstraction ladder

Every container sits at a level of abstraction, and higher levels save more.
Raw is the bottom; the closer to "drop it in and eat," the higher the leverage.

- raw onion → *diced onion* → *caramelized onion* → *sofrito base*
- dried beans → *cooked beans* → *seasoned refried beans*
- raw chicken → *poached shredded chicken* → *chicken-tinga filling*

A tub of raw diced onion saves you knife time. A tub of caramelized onion saves
you knife time *and* forty minutes at the stove *and* the only step that can't
be rushed. **Prep at the highest level that stays flexible.** Go too high and
you've cooked a finished dish (a single-use meal, not a reusable block); the
sweet spot is the richest base that still composes into many things.

This is the function-to-framework move: one-off effort, captured once, becomes a
primitive you write future meals *against*. You stop foraging raw ingredients
mid-cook and start calling a library you already built.

## Build a composable set

Primitives are worth most when they combine. A shelf of caramelized onions,
garlic confit, cooked grains, a roasted-veg medley, and a poached protein isn't
five leftovers — it's a grammar. Grain + veg + protein + a sauce is a bowl.
Onion + confit + eggs is a frittata. The same blocks compose into a week of
different dinners. Design your prep so the pieces snap together, not so you have
five unrelated finished things.

## Extend your portion, don't modify the base

A base is open for extension, closed for modification. You take a scoop of the
caramelized onions and finish *that scoop* however tonight's dish wants — into
soup, onto a burger, folded into eggs. What you do not do is re-season the whole
tub to suit one meal. The moment you spike the entire batch with cumin for
tonight's curry, it is no longer a neutral base — every future dish inherits a
modification it never asked for, and your reusable primitive has collapsed into
a single-purpose ingredient. Build to the portion; never reach back and mutate
the shared base. The block stays general precisely because you extend copies of
it instead of editing the original.

## Label it — the interface contract

An unlabeled container cannot be composed, because you don't know its interface.
What is it, and when was it made? No label means you reverse-engineer it by
sniffing, or you don't trust it and it rots into a
`supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry` write-off. **Every container gets a name
and a date.** The label is the function signature; without it the block is
unusable no matter how good its contents.

## Assets, not debt

This skill is the positive inverse of `supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry`.
That skill clears liabilities; this one builds **assets** — capital you invest
on a slow Sunday that pays a dividend every rushed weeknight. Same shelf,
opposite sign. A fridge full of composable bases is wealth; a fridge full of
mystery tubs is debt. The difference is entirely whether you prepped for
composition and labeled the interface.

It also feeds `supper-powers:bake-or-bakery`: a base you containerized once is
now a *buy* for every future meal — you already paid the build cost, so grabbing
it off the shelf is free. Building primitives is how you turn tomorrow's
expensive builds into cheap buys.

## Anti-Rationalization

- "I'll just cook it fresh when I need it." → Then you pay the full cook cost
  every single time, including the slow steps, on the nights you have the least
  time. Invest the batch once; draw down all week.
- "I prepped a bunch of stuff, that counts." → Did you prep *composable* bases,
  or five finished dishes that only become themselves? Prepping at the wrong
  level — too raw to save real time, or too finished to recombine — is motion,
  not leverage. Aim for the richest base that still snaps into many meals.
- "I'll remember what's in it." → You won't, and future-you trusts nothing
  unlabeled. An unlabeled block is a non-composable block. Name it and date it
  or you didn't really containerize it.
- "More prep is always better." → No — a shelf of primitives you never compose
  is speculative generality: a framework no app uses, slowly spoiling. Build
  the blocks you'll actually draw on this week, not every block you can imagine.

**About to put food away without deciding what it composes into and labeling
it? Stop. Pick its level, name it, date it.** A container that can't be
composed is just a leftover with ambition. Violating the letter of this rule is
violating the spirit of it.

## Boundary

This skill builds reusable prep primitives ahead of time. It does not run the
per-cook station setup for a single meal (`supper-powers:using-mise-en-place`,
its same-day cousin), it does not decide whether to build or buy a given base
(`supper-powers:bake-or-bakery`), and it does not triage primitives that have
aged into debt (`supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry`). Stay in scope: batch a
composable, labeled base at the right level of abstraction.

## User instructions always win

If your human partner says "don't batch-prep, I just want tonight's dinner" —
cook tonight's dinner. The user is in control. You can note one high-leverage
batch once ("while the oven's on, doubling the roasted veg would set up two more
meals"), then drop it. Their kitchen, their call.
