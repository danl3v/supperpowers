# Bake or Bakery

**Activates when any component could be built or bought**, before you commit the
time to building it. Two cooks fail here in opposite directions, and both think
they're doing it right.

The **over-baker** makes everything from scratch and serves dinner at 11pm —
four hours of homemade stock for a Tuesday soup nobody clocked. The
**over-buyer** stacks three store-bought layers and calls it homemade lasagna,
paying convenience tax on the one component that was supposed to carry the dish.
The skill is telling which is which, every time, on purpose.

## The Iron Law

**Build what's the point. Buy the commodity.** The labor goes where the eater
can taste it. Spending your scratch-budget on a component no one will notice is
the same mistake as buying the component the whole dish rests on — just
backwards.

## The decision

For each component, ask in order:

1. **Is this component the point of the dish?**
   If the sauce *is* the meal, build the sauce. If it's the thing they'll
   remember, it's worth your hours. Build.
2. **Do I need control the bought version won't give me?**
   Allergen, salt level, spice, dietary constraint, a specific texture. If the
   carton can't meet the spec from `supper-powers:brainstorming-supper`, build.
3. **Can anyone actually taste the difference?**
   Canned beans, dried pasta, store puff pastry, stock in a low-stakes braise —
   if a blind eater couldn't pick yours out, **buy.** Pride is not a flavor.
4. **What's the time budget?**
   Even a worth-building component gets bought when the clock can't afford it.
   A great dish served is better than a perfect dish abandoned. Buy and move on.
5. **Will I reuse it / is it cheaper at my volume?**
   Building amortizes when you'll use it again this week or it's much cheaper in
   bulk. One-off specialty build for a single use is usually a buy. (And the
   bought single-use jar is a `supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry` write-off
   waiting to happen — factor that in.)

If you're still unsure after these: **buy.** Building is the expensive,
irreversible option (you can't un-spend the two hours). Default to the cheap,
reversible one and spend your build-budget where the answer was a clear yes.

## It's all tradeoffs — and time is the one you keep forgetting

Build-vs-buy is never free on either side; you are always trading something.
Build trades time and effort for control and quality. Buy trades money and a
little control for **time** — and time is the resource cooks systematically
undervalue, because the bill comes due all at once on the night you're already
behind. Buying the stock, the pastry, the rotisserie chicken is not a moral
failure or a compromise of standards. It is a deliberate purchase of the
scarcest thing in the kitchen: hours you don't have. A cook with three hours and
a cook with thirty minutes should make *different* build-vs-buy calls on the
exact same menu, and both are right. Name the trade out loud — "I'm buying back
forty minutes here at the cost of a few dollars and a little control" — and the
decision gets easy. The mistake isn't buying; it's pretending time wasn't on the
table.

## Commodities are substitutable; the dish shouldn't care which one

When something is a true commodity, any equivalent stands in for any other
without breaking the recipe. Vegetable, canola, or sunflower oil in a neutral
fry; chicken stock from this brand or that; one all-purpose flour for another.
If the dish only depends on "a neutral oil," it must not secretly break when you
swap one neutral oil for another — if it does, you were lying to yourself about
that being a commodity, and the real dependency was more specific than you
admitted. The test of a genuine commodity is exactly this substitutability:
hand the recipe any conforming version and it still works. This is what lets you
buy the commodity with confidence — you're depending on the *role* (a neutral
fat, an acid, a stock), not on one particular jar. Where a swap *does* break the
dish — toasted sesame oil is not a neutral oil, it's a flavor — that ingredient
was never the commodity; it's a real, specific dependency, and it belongs back
in question 1.

**Know the framework, not just the recipe.** Substitutability isn't only about
tidiness — it's about resilience, and that resilience lives in *you*, not the
recipe card. A cook who knows only the recipe ("¼ cup vegetable oil") is brittle:
the oil runs out mid-fry, the called-for stock is off, the herb is wilted, and
they're stuck, because the recipe never told them *why* that ingredient was there
or what could stand in. A cook who understands the framework — this step needs a
neutral fat at frying temperature; this needs an acid; this needs a savory
liquid — can swap in real time without breaking stride. Recipes encode the happy
path. Frameworks survive when a dependency goes bad or missing, which it will,
usually at the worst moment. Learn the role each ingredient plays, not just its
name, so that when the substitution is forced on you, you already know what
conforms. The brittle cook follows steps; the resilient cook understands roles.

## When you buy, read the label: fewer, known ingredients

A "buy" is pulling in a dependency, and every bought component drags in
whatever *its* ingredients are — transitively, into your dish. So vet the
dependency before you add it. Prefer the bought item with the **shortest, most
legible ingredient list.** A jarred sauce of tomatoes, oil, salt, and basil is
a dependency you understand; the same sauce with a dozen stabilizers,
flavor-maskers, and additives you can't pronounce is a dependency whose effects
you can't predict — extra sweetness that throws your seasoning, a gum that
changes the texture downstream, an allergen hiding three names deep.

The fewer and more known the ingredients, the fewer unknown effects propagate
into the dish you're actually responsible for. An unfamiliar additive is an
untrusted transitive dependency: it might be fine, but you didn't audit it, and
when the final dish tastes off you won't know which imported component did it.
Between two buys that meet the spec, take the one with less in it. A short
ingredient list is a small dependency footprint, and a small footprint is
fewer surprises in `supper-powers:test-driven-dinner` later.

A long ingredient list is also a larger attack surface. Each additive you can't
vet is a way the dish can fail in a way you didn't choose — the hidden allergen
that sends someone to the hospital, the preservative someone reacts to, the
"natural flavors" that clash with everything. You are importing not just taste
but risk, and risk you didn't audit is the risk that bites you.

## But sometimes the risky dependency is the right call

Minimal footprint is the default, not a religion. Some processed, long-list,
"impure" dependencies are *the correct choice* because they do something no
clean-label build can. American cheese is an emulsified, additive-laden
industrial product — and it melts into a sauce nothing artisanal can match.
That's why it's on the burger. Oreos are a list of things you'd never make at
home, and they are *perfect*; no from-scratch chocolate sandwich cookie is
actually better at being an Oreo. Boxed gelatin, instant dashi, good
hot sauce, MSG — accepting the dependency buys a result the pure path can't
reach.

The rule is not "always minimize." The rule is **take on the dependency
deliberately, because it's the right tool, never by default because it's the
easy one.** A vetted risk you chose for a real payoff is good engineering. The
same additive reached for out of laziness, when a clean build would've served
the dish better, is debt. Same ingredient, opposite decision — what separates
them is whether you picked it on purpose. Know what you're importing, know why,
and decide it's worth it. Then enjoy the American cheese without guilt.

## The canonical cases

- **Stock:** homemade is genuinely better — but the carton is the right call on
  a weeknight braise where it's a background note. Build it when the soup *is*
  stock; buy it when stock is scaffolding.
- **Flour:** nobody is impressed that you didn't mill your own. Buy. There is
  always a deeper "from scratch" and the point is to stop at the level that
  matters, not the bottom.
- **The birthday cake:** the one place store-bought *lands differently*. Here
  the homemade-ness is itself the point — the wonk is the love. Build, even if
  the bakery's is technically better.

That last one is the whole skill in miniature: "better" is measured against the
dish's actual goal, not against some absolute. Sometimes scratch wins on
meaning, not on taste.

## Anti-Rationalization

- "Real cooks make it from scratch." → Real cooks ship dinner. Scratch for its
  own sake is vanity, and vanity is the over-baker's 11pm soup. Build for the
  eater, not for your self-image.
- "Buying it feels like cheating." → It's cheating the same way using a library
  instead of writing your own is cheating: it isn't. You bought leverage so you
  could spend effort where it counts. That's the job.
- "I've already got the ingredients, might as well build." → Sunk-cost talking.
  The question is the *next* hour, not the flour already in the bag. If the
  component isn't the point, buy and save the hour.
- "I always make this myself." → Habit is not a decision. Re-run the four
  questions for *this* meal's time budget and goal. Sometimes the answer flips.

**About to build a component you haven't checked against the four questions?
Stop. Run them.** Building is the irreversible spend; justify it before you
commit the time. Violating the letter of this rule is violating the spirit of it.

## Boundary

This skill decides build-vs-buy for a single component. It does not set the
dish's goal (`supper-powers:brainstorming-supper`, which this feeds as a
sub-decision before heat), it does not cook the component once you've chosen to
build (`supper-powers:test-driven-dinner`), and it does not manage the
single-use ingredients a "buy" leaves behind (`supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry`).
Stay in scope: is this one the point, or a commodity?

## User instructions always win

If your human partner says "just buy the pastry, I don't care" — buy the pastry.
The user is in control. You can flag one case once where building clearly serves
the goal ("worth noting the cake's the kind that's better homemade"), then
defer. Their kitchen, their call.
