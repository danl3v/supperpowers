# Seasoning to Taste

**Activates whenever you're salting or balancing flavor** — it's the technique
that the GREEN step of `supper-powers:test-driven-dinner` calls when the test is
"under-seasoned." That skill tells you *to* correct in small steps and re-taste;
this one tells you *how* to correct well and how to recover when you overshoot.

## The Iron Law

**Season up in small steps; you can always add, you can't subtract.** Salt is a
one-way commit until you do real work to reverse it. Approach the edge from
below. Never dump.

## Where the edge actually is

A dish is oversalted when **you can taste the salt itself** — when "salt" reads
as its own flavor rather than disappearing into the food and making everything
else louder. That's the ceiling, and it's sharp: cross it and the dish tastes
*of salt* instead of *seasoned*.

But the threshold is more forgiving than nervous cooks think. Properly seasoned
food sits surprisingly close to the line — well short of "tastes salty," but
further up than timid cooks dare to go. Under-salting is the far more common
error, and it has no flavor of its own; it just reads as flat, dull, "missing
something." Most dishes that feel like they need *more* of some mystery
ingredient simply need salt. Be braver than you think on the way up, and stop
the moment salt becomes audible.

## Salt makes everything else louder

Salt's real job isn't to taste salty — it's to amplify. The right amount makes
the tomato more tomato, the stock more savory, the sweetness rounder. That's why
"flat" is the under-salted signature: the other flavors are there but quiet. You
are turning up a volume knob, and you stop just before the knob's own hiss
(salt-as-flavor) becomes audible.

## Rescuing an over-correction

You overshot. Do **not** start over or throw it out — that's the panic move, and
seasoning is recoverable. You can't remove the salt, but you can rebalance
around it:

- **Add volume — the dilution fix.** More unsalted base spreads the same salt
  across more food. Add unsalted stock, water, more of the vegetables, a second
  batch's worth of the bland component. The total salt is unchanged; the
  concentration drops. This is the most reliable rescue when you have room to
  scale the dish up.
- **Add acid — the balance fix.** A squeeze of lemon, a splash of vinegar.
  Acid doesn't lower the salt, but it re-balances the palate so the saltiness
  reads as seasoned-and-bright rather than just salty. Often the fastest fix for
  a dish that's only slightly over.
- **Add fat or sweetness — the round-off fix.** A knob of butter, a little
  cream, a pinch of sugar can blunt a sharp saline edge by coating it and
  competing for attention. Use sparingly; you're balancing, not building a new
  dish.
- **Serve it against a blank.** Plate the too-salty thing over plain rice,
  bread, or unsalted starch that soaks up and spreads the seasoning at the table.

(The potato myth — dropping a raw potato in to "absorb" salt — barely works;
it removes salty *liquid* by volume, no more than scooping would. Reach for
dilution, acid, or fat instead.)

## This is balance, not just salt

Salt is the main lever but the same logic runs the whole palate: chase what's
*missing*, not more of what's already there. Flat → salt. Flat and dull even
with salt → acid. Harsh or bitter → fat or a little sweet. Thin → more
reduction or umami. Taste, name the gap, add the smallest thing that closes it,
re-taste. Same RED-GREEN loop, finer instrument.

## Anti-Rationalization

- "I'll salt it all at the end." → Seasoning in layers as you cook tastes
  deeper than one late dump, and the late dump is where oversalting happens.
  Season along the way, in steps, tasting each time.
- "It tastes flat, but I'm scared to add more salt." → Flat *is* the symptom of
  too little. You're likely under, not over. Add a small pinch and re-taste —
  the edge is further up than your nerves suggest.
- "It's too salty, the dish is ruined." → It's recoverable. Dilute with volume,
  brighten with acid, or round with fat before you ever consider starting over.
  Panic throws out salvageable food.
- "More salt fixed it last time, so more is better." → Up to the edge, yes;
  past it, no. The edge is "you can taste the salt." Stop there, every time.

**About to add a big pour of salt, or about to dump a dish you oversalted?
Stop.** Add salt in pinches toward the edge; rescue overshoot with volume, acid,
or fat. Violating the letter of this rule is violating the spirit of it.

## Boundary

This skill is the seasoning technique invoked by the GREEN step of
`supper-powers:test-driven-dinner`; that skill owns the taste-correct-retaste
*loop*, this one owns *how* to season and rescue within it. It does not decide
the dish (`supper-powers:brainstorming-supper`) or gate the final plate
(`supper-powers:requesting-table-review`). Stay in scope: season to the edge,
balance the palate, recover overshoot.

## User instructions always win

If your human partner keeps their food well under-seasoned by preference, or
can't have much salt for health reasons — season to *their* edge, not the
textbook one. The user is in control. Offer acid or other lifts to compensate
for flavor if salt is limited, then cook to their taste. Their kitchen, their
call.
