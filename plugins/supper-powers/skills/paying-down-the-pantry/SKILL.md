# Paying Down the Pantry

**Activates when planning a cook, before you commit to a menu.** Every kitchen
carries debt: things a past version of you left for a future version of you to
deal with. That future version is now. The debt was invisible when you incurred
it and it is taxing you today — the cluttered counter you cook slower on, the
$11 ingredient guilt-tripping you from the shelf, the freezer you can't close.

This is not a cleaning skill. It is a **triage** skill. You are not scrubbing
everything; you are finding what's there, pricing it, and paying down the bits
that buy you the most speed back.

## The Iron Law

**Audit before you plan.** You cannot cook around debt you haven't found. Open
the doors and look *before* you decide what to make, because what's back there
should change what you make.

## The four kinds of pantry debt

1. **Deferred-cleanup debt** — the stuff you didn't clean last cook. The pan
   "soaking" since Tuesday, the sticky shelf, the knife that needs honing. This
   is literal interest: it makes today's cook slower and grosser, and it
   compounds the longer it sits (soaking turns to crusted turns to scrubbing).

2. **Impulse-buy debt** — the weird ingredient you bought with big plans. The
   gochujang, the za'atar, the tin of anchovies, the dragonfruit. Unopened,
   unamortized, slowly aging into a write-off. You paid for it; you owe it a use.

3. **Freezer-archaeology debt** — the strata at the back of the chest freezer.
   The unlabeled foil brick. The half-bag of peas from an unknowable era. The
   portion of soup you were so proud of. Capital you froze and forgot; it only
   pays out if you go excavate it.

4. **Smashed-chip-bag debt** — the physical clutter wedged in wrong. The
   crushed chip bag jammed in the cupboard, the avalanche shelf, the lid
   drawer that won't open. Pure friction. It doesn't even feed you; it just
   slows every reach.

## The audit: find it and price it

Open the doors and take inventory — fridge, freezer, pantry, the counter you've
been cooking around. For each piece of debt, price it on two axes:

- **Interest** — how much is this slowing me down / how fast is it decaying?
  The soaking pan blocks the sink *every* cook (high). The shelf-stable za'atar
  ages slowly (low).
- **Payoff cost** — how much effort to clear it? Tossing the expired thing is
  cheap; defrosting and identifying the foil brick is dear.

## Triage: pay the cheap, high-interest debt first

You are not clearing all of it today. Rank and pick:

- **Pay now (high interest, cheap):** scrub the pan blocking your only sink,
  toss the chip bag so the cupboard opens, bin the obviously-dead produce.
  Minutes of work, immediate speed back. Do these before you cook.
- **Cook it down (the using-up move):** when an impulse buy or freezer find is
  decaying *and* usable, the best payment is to **build today's menu around it.**
  This is where the skill feeds `supper-powers:brainstorming-supper`: "the brief
  is, use the gochujang and the freezer pork before they turn." Debt paid by
  eating it.
- **Log for next cook (low interest):** the shelf-stable oddity, the cluttered
  but-not-blocking drawer. Write it on the next-cook list and move on. Not
  every debt is due today.
- **Write it off (sunk):** the unidentifiable, the freezer-burned, the long
  expired. The money is already gone — keeping it costs you space and decision
  energy with zero return. Toss it without ceremony. Holding a write-off on the
  books helps no one.

## The compounding warning

Pantry debt is the REFACTOR step of `supper-powers:test-driven-dinner` skipped
one too many times. Every cook where you don't clean the station, don't use the
aging thing, don't clear the clutter, you borrow a little speed from your future
self at a brutal interest rate. Skip it for a month and you get the kitchen
everyone has had: full of food, nothing to cook, no counter to cook it on. A
little payment every cook keeps the balance from ever getting there.

## Anti-Rationalization

- "I'll deal with that pan later." → Later is now, and now you have less time
  than last-you did. Deferred-cleanup debt is the one that compounds hardest.
  Pay the high-interest items before you start.
- "I'm saving that ingredient for the right dish." → The right dish is not
  coming and the ingredient is aging. Today's dish is the right dish. Use it
  or you'll write it off.
- "I don't have time to audit, I just need to cook." → You don't have time
  *not* to — you're about to cook around debt that'll slow this whole session.
  The audit is two minutes of opening doors and it reshapes the menu for free.
- "I might still use that." → If you can't name the dish and the date, it's a
  write-off pretending to be inventory. Toss it; reclaim the shelf.

## Boundary

This skill finds, prices, and triages existing kitchen debt, and it may feed a
"use this up" brief into `supper-powers:brainstorming-supper`. It does not plan
the menu itself, it does not do the per-cook station reset (that's the REFACTOR
step of `supper-powers:test-driven-dinner`, which *prevents* new
deferred-cleanup debt), and it does not cook. Stay in scope: audit, price,
pay down or write off.

## User instructions always win

If your human partner says "don't reorganize my freezer, just make dinner" —
make dinner. The user is in control. You can flag one high-interest item once
("heads up, that pan's blocking the sink"), then leave the rest. Their kitchen,
their call.
