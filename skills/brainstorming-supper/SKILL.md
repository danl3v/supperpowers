# Brainstorming Supper

**Activates before a single ingredient is touched.** The cardinal sin is the
eager cook who hears "I'm hungry" and immediately starts searing. Searing what?
For whom? You don't know yet. Put the pan down.

## The Iron Law

**No prep starts until there is an agreed menu.** Heat is a commitment. Once
the onion hits the oil you have foreclosed options. Stay in the cheap phase —
talking — for as long as it's still cheap.

## What you are doing

You are turning a vague craving into a spec a cook could actually execute. A
craving is "something warm." A spec is "a brothy noodle bowl, 25 minutes, uses
the bok choy before it wilts, no shellfish." You get from one to the other by
asking, not assuming.

## Questions to tease out the spec

Ask the few that actually matter for *this* meal. Don't interrogate.

- **Who's eating?** Headcount, and anyone with a hard constraint — allergy,
  diet, the kid who won't touch sauce. Hard constraints are non-negotiable;
  surface them now, not at the table.
- **What's the time budget?** Twenty minutes on a weeknight and "I've got all
  afternoon" are different programs. Don't design a braise for a 20-minute slot.
- **What must get used?** The wilting herbs, the open can, the leftover rice.
  Cooking around what's perishable is free value. (See
  `supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry` — sometimes the brief *is* "use up
  the back of the fridge.")
- **What's the mood?** Comfort, light, impressive, restorative. This picks the
  whole direction more than any single ingredient does.
- **What's actually on hand?** Don't design a dish that needs a 9pm store run
  unless they've signed up for one.

## Explore alternatives — don't commit to the first idea

The first dish that comes to mind is a starting point, not a decision. Offer a
couple of genuinely different directions and let the eater react:

> "I'm hearing comfort + 30 minutes + use the mushrooms. That could go
> creamy — a mushroom pasta — or brothy — a mushroom-and-noodle soup. Or we
> lean savory-crisp and do mushroom quesadillas. Which way?"

People know what they want when they see the fork in the road, even when they
couldn't name it cold. The alternatives do the work.

## Present the menu in chunks for sign-off

Don't dump a six-paragraph plan. Show it in pieces small enough to react to:
the dish, then the rough components, then the sequence. Get a "yes, that"
before moving on. The output of this skill is an **agreed menu** — the recipe.

## Anti-Rationalization

- "I already know what they want." → Then confirming costs you one sentence
  and a nod. Confirm anyway. The cost of guessing wrong is a whole wasted cook.
- "Asking is annoying, I'll just make something good." → Good *for whom*? A
  perfect dish the eater didn't want is a failed dish. Hunger has a target.
- "We can just adjust while cooking." → Some choices don't reverse. You can't
  un-braise into a stir-fry. Direction is cheap now and expensive later.
- "It's just a quick lunch, no need to plan." → Quick is exactly when a wrong
  turn hurts most, because there's no slack to recover in. One question still
  beats a redo.

**Started cooking without an agreed menu? Stop. Put the pan down. Get the
yes first.** Violating the letter of this rule is violating the spirit of it.

## Boundary

This skill ends at an agreed menu. It does not prep the station (that's
`supper-powers:using-mise-en-place`), it does not cook or season (that's
`supper-powers:test-driven-dinner`), and it does not decide what to use up
from storage (that's `supper-powers:paying-down-the-pantry`, which may *feed*
this conversation). Stay in scope: ask, explore, agree.

## User instructions always win

If your human partner says "stop asking, just make the pasta" — make the pasta.
The user is in control. You can name one assumption you're making ("going with
garlic-and-oil since you didn't say"), then cook. Their kitchen, their call.
